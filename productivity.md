# Productivity Strategies

## Scheduling
- **Personal office hours**: have designated time(s) when people can come to you with brief questions, rather than requiring them to schedule a one-off meeting.
  - Sometimes, they will be able to figure out the answer between now and the next office hours.
- **No meeting day**: make space for focus time by disallowing meetings on a particular day of the week, except for emergencies ([details](https://calnewport.com/why-are-maker-schedules-so-rare/)).
  - This often requires buy-in from the rest of your immediate team. But they will probably like it too.
- **Decline meetings without an agenda**: politely decline meetings added to your calendar that lack an agenda.
  - If someone can't be bothered to spend 15 seconds writing a sentence about the purpose of the meeting, is it really worth spending 30 minutes talking with them?
- **Automatically decline meetings during blocked-off time**: when possible, have the calendar software automatically decline meeting invitations that lie during focus time, when out of office, etc.
  - The fact that this is immediate and automatic prevents the decline from feeling like a spiteful, personal rejection.

## Task Management
- **Centralize things to remember**: avoid the chronic stress of trying to remember what you were going to do.
  - The consistency of using this centralized repository matters more than what that system is e.g. to-do app, notes document, text file, handwritten notebook.
- **Centralize project management**: use a shared project board (e.g. Trello, Asana, Jira) to organize work.
  - This project board eliminates the need for status updates.
  - Individuals can _pull_ work from the board asynchronously, rather than messaging someone to ask for a new task.

## Communication
- **You don't need to respond immediately**:
  - People often figure things out with just a little bit of time and resourcefulness. Many times folks have sent me a Slack DM with a question, and then within 20 minutes they reply "Nevermind, I figured it out."
  - Oftentimes, just want reassurance that you received their message and are planning to reply. You can set expectations: "I go through all of these approvals every Friday morning."
- **Prefer public communication channels over private**: public conversations allow others to respond and allow others to learn from the answer.
  - Private messages, by contrast, disallow participation and silo the answers.
  - For example, in Slack, redirect DMs to public channels. This works best as a team-wide policy.
- **Write messages to only require a single correspondence**: instead of conversational back-and-forth, reply with multiple choices so that the recipient can close the loop in a single response ([details](https://calnewport.com/deep-habits-write-your-own-e-mail-protocols/)).
  - Wrong way (6 messages): "Want to get coffee next week?" -> "Sure" -> "How about Monday?" -> "That works, I'm free until 11am" -> "10am then?" -> "Sure"
  - Right way (2 messages): "Want to get coffee next week? I'm available on Monday at 9am and 10am, Tuesday at 10am, and Wednesday at 11am." -> "Monday at 10am works"
- **Create project-specific communication channels**: create a dedicated email inbox or Slack channel for projects.
  - People sending a message to person@domain.com are more likely interpret a delay as a personal affront, whereas project@domain.com avoids that because the inbox is not attached to an individual person.

**Routines and Rituals**
- **Time-block plan**: assign tasks to time on the calendar, rather than looking at a simple to-do list.
  - This avoids the trap of getting into the groove of focused work just before some distracting meetings.
  - Sometimes this can also reveal opportunities to consolidate, postpone, or cancel meetings.
- **Adopt a shutdown ritual**: performing this routine at the end of the work day to reduce stress.
  - Reviewing tasks and taking note of what needs to be done next prevents stress from spreading into off-work hours.
